1. A 43-year-old woman undergoes diagnostic mammography for focal asymmetry. DBT reveals architectural distortion, BI-RADS 4A. Ultrasound is negative. Core biopsy of the distortion shows benign fibrocystic change. The radiologist considers imaging-pathology concordant. Should short-interval follow-up or surgical excision be recommended?  
   1. ​​Surgical excision of the biopsied site because architectural distortion has an unacceptably high underestimation rate regardless of concordance.  
   2. Short-interval imaging follow-up (e.g., 6-month diagnostic mammography/DBT) because the biopsy is concordant and the lesion was low-suspicion (4A).  
   3. Immediate breast MRI to exclude occult malignancy, followed by excision if MRI is positive; no follow-up if MRI is negative.  
   4. Repeat percutaneous biopsy with vacuum-assisted device because benign fibrocystic change is not a sufficiently specific benign correlate for distortion, even if deemed concordant.  
   5. No additional imaging or intervention until routine annual screening because benign concordant pathology upgrades are negligible in 4A distortion.

Answer: B

2. A 51-year-old woman has a BI-RADS 3 lesion measuring approximately 6 mm, which has been stable for 18 months. After 24 months, it shows minimal enlargement (1 mm) but identical morphology. Should this be upgraded to BI-RADS 4 or can monitoring continue?  
   1. Upgrade to BI-RADS 4A because any interval growth at any time during BI-RADS 3 surveillance constitutes a suspicious change warranting biopsy.  
   2. Continue BI-RADS 3 surveillance because a 1 mm change at this size is within expected measurement variability if morphology is unchanged; return to routine screening after completing the BI-RADS 3 protocol.  
   3. Reclassify as BI-RADS 2 because stability of morphology over 24 months outweighs minimal size change; no further targeted follow-up is indicated.  
   4. Keep BI-RADS 3 but shorten the interval (e.g., repeat targeted US/mammo in 6 months) and explicitly document measurement technique/planes; upgrade only if growth is confirmed on reproducible measurements or morphology changes.  
   5. Upgrade to BI-RADS 4B because in women \>50, even minimal interval growth increases pretest probability enough that surveillance is no longer appropriate.

	Answer: D

3. A 38-year-old with extremely dense breasts has a screening MRI showing a 6 mm non-mass enhancement, BI-RADS 4\. MRI-guided biopsy yields benign proliferative changes. What factors determine whether this is concordant?  
   1. Concordant if the biopsy shows benign proliferative change without atypia, because any proliferative lesion can enhance; discordance mainly applies to BI-RADS 5 or upgrade-risk histology.  
   2. Concordant if the NME is very small and focal with non–washout kinetics, since missed cancers are uncommon at 6 mm; discordance is reserved for larger or segmental disease.  
   3. Concordant only if the benign diagnosis plausibly explains the specific NME morphology/kinetics and the procedure documents accurate targeting and adequate sampling of the enhancing tissue.  
   4. Concordant if technical adequacy is demonstrated—clip at the target, post-biopsy change in enhancement, sufficient cores/vacuum sampling—regardless of whether pathology is nonspecific.  
   5. Concordant is determined mainly by clinical context: age, density, hormones, and calculated lifetime risk; higher pretest probability makes benign proliferative change inherently discordant.

   Answer: C

      

4. A screening mammogram shows clustered calcifications BI-RADS 4B. Stereotactic core biopsy samples calcifications, pathology reports sclerosing adenosis with calcifications. Is this automatically concordant?  
   1. Yes — sclerosing adenosis is a benign explanation for calcifications, so this is automatically concordant if calcium is present in the specimen.  
   2. Yes — BI-RADS 4B implies an intermediate PPV, and any benign proliferative lesion is concordant if imaging targeting was correct.  
   3. Yes, but only if the biopsy used a vacuum-assisted device with ≥12 cores; otherwise it is automatically discordant.  
   4. No — any BI-RADS 4B calcifications require surgical excision regardless of benign core pathology.  
   5. No — it is not automatically concordant; concordance depends on whether the histology plausibly explains the *morphology and distribution* of the calcifications and the level of suspicion, and may still be discordant/high-risk depending on imaging features and sampling adequacy.

Answer: E

5. A 2.5 cm spiculated mass on mammography yields core biopsy diagnosis of “fibrosis with chronic inflammation.” Radiologist documents “possible sampling error.” Pathologist notes adequate cores. Who determines discordance and what is the next step?  
   1. Pathologist determines discordance based on tissue adequacy; issue addendum confirming concordance and return to routine screening.  
   2. Radiologist (in multidisciplinary correlation with pathology) determines discordance; recommend repeat tissue sampling (preferably surgical excision or repeat biopsy targeting the imaging abnormality).  
   3. Radiologist determines discordance; because pathology shows benign inflammation and cores are adequate, classify as concordant benign and perform 6-month diagnostic mammographic follow-up without re-biopsy.  
   4. Surgeon determines discordance after clinical exam; short-interval (6-month) imaging follow-up is appropriate because cores are adequate.  
   5. Tumor board determines discordance only if malignancy is proven; order breast MRI first, then decide whether to biopsy again.  
   6. Discussion in tumorboard; The radiologist must re-examine the results and decide whether the radiological and pathological findings match. If there is still a discrepancy, another open excision should be performed.

	Answer:B

6. Ultrasound-guided biopsy of a 1.8 cm hypoechoic irregular mass returns LCIS. Imaging appearance is highly suspicious for invasion. How should this discordance be managed?  
   1. Treat as concordant because LCIS is a high-risk lesion; recommend 6-month ultrasound follow-up only  
   2. Repeat ultrasound-guided core biopsy with a larger gauge needle and accept results if LCIS is again found  
   3. Manage with surgical excision or vacuum-assisted excision if appropriate to resolve discordance and exclude invasive cancer  
   4. Begin endocrine risk-reduction therapy (e.g., tamoxifen) without additional tissue sampling  
   5. Obtain breast MRI first; if MRI shows no enhancement, no further intervention is needed

	Answer: C

7. A lesion visible only on MRI is biopsied with a vacuum device. Pathology shows ADH. The enhancing area measured 2 cm. Does lesion size affect management?  
   1. Lesion size does **not** influence management; all MRI-only ADH diagnosed on VAB can be safely observed with 6-month MRI follow-up.  
   2. Lesion size matters; ADH on VAB from a 2 cm MRI enhancing area carries a higher upgrade risk, so surgical excision is generally recommended, particularly if radiologic–pathologic concordance is not unequivocal.  
   3. Lesion size matters only if calcifications are present on mammography; for MRI-only lesions, size has no impact on upgrade risk.  
   4. For MRI-only lesions, smaller lesions have higher upgrade risk due to sampling error, so excision is preferred only when the enhancing area is \<1 cm.  
   5. Management should be dictated solely by the vacuum device gauge and number of cores; lesion size is irrelevant if ≥12 cores are obtained.  
   6. No, the treatment usually consists of complete excision. If the suspicious lesion has been completely removed during imaging by means of a biopsy, open excision is not necessary. Open excision is indicated for a finding of 2 cm.

Answer: B

8. Core biopsy of a 1.1 cm lesion reports “invasive carcinoma, grade 1”.Surgical excision reveals grade 3 with lymphovascular invasion. What are the known limitations of grading on core biopsy?  
   1. Core biopsy grade is frequently upgraded at excision because sampling captures only a small, potentially low-grade portion of a heterogeneous tumor; mitotic count is often underestimated on cores; and overall grade shows only moderate concordance with excision—especially for intermediate/high-grade components.  
   2. Core biopsy grade is unreliable mainly because formalin fixation artifacts routinely inflate nuclear pleomorphism, leading to systematic overgrading; therefore excision grading is usually lower than core grade.  
   3. Core biopsy grade is invalid because the Nottingham (Elston–Ellis) system cannot be applied to small samples; grading should only be performed on excisions where tumor size exceeds 2 cm.  
   4. Discordance is best explained because lymphovascular invasion automatically increases Nottingham grade and is commonly missed on core biopsy.  
   5. Core biopsy grade is limited primarily by inability to determine tubule formation on cores, whereas nuclear pleomorphism and mitotic activity are typically identical to excision, so true upgrading is rare.

	Answer: A

9. The core biopsy revealed DCIS, high grade, comedonecrosis in a 50-year-old female patient. The final pathology shows invasive carcinoma with a 1.5 mm focus pT1a. Should a sentinel lymph node biopsy have been performed first?  
   1. Yes—always do SLNB for any DCIS because occult invasion is common.  
   2. Yes—high-grade DCIS with comedonecrosis mandates SLNB regardless of the planned breast operation.  
   3. No—never do SLNB for DCIS; even if a pT1a focus is later found, nodal staging is unnecessary.  
   4. Depends on the planned breast surgery (best answer): For DCIS, SLNB is not routine with lumpectomy; it is recommended when mastectomy is planned (or lymphatics will be disrupted), because SLNB can’t be reliably done later. If unexpected invasion (pT1a) is found after lumpectomy, SLNB can be considered as a second-stage procedure based on overall context.   
   5. Leaning No (INSEMA study): In a patient who ultimately looks “low-risk, cN0, BCT \+ RT” (age ≥50; small invasive focus), data from axillary de-escalation trials such as INSEMA support that omitting SLNB in selected early invasive cases can be non-inferior for key outcomes and reduces arm morbidity—so doing SLNB “just in case” for presumed DCIS is not automatically necessary. 

   Answer: D

10. A core biopsy for architectural distortion reveals radial scar without atypia. Surgical excision shows tubular carcinoma. What sampling principles were violated?  
    1. Failure to target the true imaging abnormality (architectural distortion) with stereotactic/vacuum-assisted sampling—cores were taken from adjacent benign radial scar rather than the center/spiculation of the distortion.  
    2. Inadequate sampling volume for a distortion lesion—too few cores and no large-volume (vacuum-assisted) sampling, leading to under-representation of a small infiltrative carcinoma (tubular) within/at the periphery of a radial scar.  
    3. Lack of specimen radiography for confirmation of correct tissue acquisition, resulting in inability to verify that the sampled cores corresponded to the mammographic distortion (i.e., no objective proof of “lesion-in-cores”).  
    4. Failure to apply radiologic–pathologic concordance principles—accepting “radial scar without atypia” as concordant for a suspicious architectural distortion, instead of escalating to repeat targeted biopsy or excision for discordance/high-risk concordance.  
    5. Incorrect assumption that absence of atypia excludes upgrade risk, leading to omission of additional sampling of lesion margins/periphery (where tubular carcinoma commonly resides), violating the “sample the interface and perilesional zone” principle for radial scar/distortion	

Answer: D
